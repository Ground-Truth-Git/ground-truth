# LinkedIn Launch Post - GroundTruth

---

"Democracy dies in darkness" is about press freedom.

But democracy also dies in a different kind of darkness - the personal darkness of being unable to evaluate what you are being told.

A free press in a population that cannot tell a primary source from a government press release is still vulnerable. A well-informed person who cannot trace a claim to its origin is still a target.

I've spent the past year building a framework for this problem. Not for journalists. Not for academics. For people like the ones I work with every day - executives, decision-makers, people who are already sceptical of vendor research, already suspicious of statistics that support conclusions too neatly, already wondering why every NGO, government, and media outlet seems to cite the same three sources.

The framework has a name for that last thing: **citation laundering**. It has nine other named failure modes. It has a five-band evidence rating anchored to intelligence community standards. And it has a single operating rule: whatever evidentiary standard you apply to a claim you dislike, apply identically to a claim you agree with.

I've published it as a free tool at groundtruth.netlify.app

One page. No account required. Includes a prompt you can paste into any AI assistant to run a source check on any claim, from any domain, on any side of any debate.

The framework works on climate data, on pharmaceutical research, on market forecasts, on political claims, on news you agree with and news you don't. It does not tell you what to think. It tells you how to check.

I built it because I think the ability to evaluate a claim is a civic skill - one that most of us were never explicitly taught, and one that an enormous number of well-funded interests are working hard to undermine.

The tool is free. Use it. Break it. Tell me where it fails.
