How to check if a claim is true

Stop being fooled by claims that sound true.

Every day you are told things - by vendors, headlines, experts, people online - that are built to sound convincing whether or not they are real. This is a simple, repeatable way to tell the difference. Five questions, ten warning signs, and a free tool that runs the whole check for you.

Check a claim now Learn the 5 questions

We worry about a free press dying in darkness. But there is a second darkness just as dangerous: not being able to tell, for yourself, whether what you are being told is true. A free press cannot save a person who cannot spot a manufactured claim. This is the light for that second darkness.

Start here

The five questions

This is the whole method. Ask these five questions about any claim, in any order, and you will catch the large majority of things that are designed to mislead you. Everything else on this page just explains how to answer them well.

1

What kind of proof is this, really?

Is it something you could re-test, like a measurement or an experiment? A physical record? Or is it just someone's opinion, an estimate, or a story passed along? Stronger proof is harder to fake. Weak proof dressed up as strong proof is the most common trick of all.

2

Where does it actually come from?

Follow the claim back to its original source. Not the website that repeated it - the place it started. If ten articles all repeat the same claim, but they are all quoting the same single report, that is one piece of evidence, not ten. This is the single most useful habit on this page.

3

Do the sources really agree, or just repeat each other?

Two sources only count as separate if they didn't use the same data, the same method, or the same people. Lots of voices saying the same thing means nothing if they all got it from one place. Also ask: what else could explain this? If the evidence fits several explanations equally, it proves none of them.

4

Who gains if you believe it - and what's being left out?

A claim made only by someone who profits from you believing it isn't proven - it's just asserted. And even true facts can mislead if the inconvenient ones are quietly left out. Ask what you're not being shown.

5

Have serious people tried to prove it wrong and failed?

A claim that has survived genuine, hostile attempts to break it is far stronger than one nobody has tested. Ask whether the people who would love to disprove this have tried - and what happened when they did.

Ten warning signs

What it looks like when the answers go wrong

These are the ten most common ways a claim is made to look better-supported than it is. Each one has a name, so that when you spot it, you can say exactly what is happening - to yourself, or out loud in a meeting.

SIGN 01

Repeating isn't proving

A claim gets passed from one outlet to the next, each one pointing at the last, until it feels true just because it is everywhere. But no one in the chain ever shows the original proof.

Spot it
Trace it back. If the trail loops or dead-ends without ever reaching real evidence, there is nothing underneath it.

SIGN 02

Many voices, one source

Lots of seemingly separate sources all turn out to be quoting the same single report, dataset, or statement. The voices are real. The independence is an illusion.

Spot it
Ten articles citing the same study are one piece of evidence, not ten. Check what they all point back to.

SIGN 03

Only the interested party says so

The only source for a claim is someone who gains directly if you believe it - a company, a campaign group, an official body. It might be true, but it has not yet been shown to be.

Spot it
Ask who benefits. If every source benefits from you believing it, you need an independent check.

SIGN 04

Borrowed authority

An official body is cited as proof of something it never actually ruled on. A case being accepted for hearing is treated as a verdict. A vote is treated as a fact about the world.

Spot it
Check what the body was actually asked, and what it actually decided. It is often narrower than the claim.

SIGN 05

Out of date

The claim rests on a source that has since been corrected, withdrawn, or replaced by better evidence - but the original keeps circulating as if nothing changed.

Spot it
Check whether this is still the most recent and best evidence, or whether it has been overtaken.

SIGN 06

A number with no context

A real figure is quoted with none of the context that gives it meaning - no comparison, no baseline, no explanation of how it was measured. The number may be accurate and the conclusion still wrong.

Spot it
Ask: compared to what? Measured how? What does the full picture show, not just this one figure?

SIGN 07

Fake precision

A claim is stated with exact-sounding numbers that the underlying data can't actually support - a single sharp figure pulled from something that was really just a rough estimate or a wide range.

Spot it
Check whether the original gave a range or a margin that has been quietly dropped to sound more certain.

SIGN 08

Guessing the motive

A claim about why someone did something, based only on the result - not on anything they actually said, wrote, or decided. What happened and why it happened are two separate questions.

Spot it
Ask what direct evidence of intention exists, separate from the outcome itself.

SIGN 09

One person, treated as everyone

A single person's comment is presented as the position of an entire company, group, or government - even when that person had no authority to speak for all of them.

Spot it
Ask whether this is official policy, or just one individual's words being made to stand for the whole.

SIGN 10

Different rules for different sides

A source is trusted when it supports one side and dismissed when it supports the other - using standards that quietly change depending on the answer they produce.

Spot it
Ask: would I accept this same source if it backed the opposite conclusion? If not, your standard is the problem.

Question 1, in detail

Not all proof is equal

When you ask "what kind of proof is this?", this is the ladder you are placing it on. The higher up, the harder it is to fake and the more confident you can be. The lower down, the more it is just a starting point that needs checking.

Strongest

Something you can re-test

An experiment or measurement anyone can run again and get the same result - a lab test, a repeated study. The strongest kind, because re-testing it removes any need to trust the person reporting it.

Very strong

Physical evidence

Something that exists in the world independently of anyone's account - forensic results, dated imagery, an authenticated document. It can't be quietly changed by someone who wants a different answer.

Strong

A record made at the time

Documents created as events happened, by people who were there - contracts, official records, transcripts. Stronger than something reconstructed from memory long afterwards.

Moderate

A reasoned conclusion

A conclusion worked out from solid evidence using clear logic. Only as good as both the evidence under it and the reasoning - and the reasoning has to be shown, not just asserted.

Use with care

Eyewitness accounts

Direct accounts from people who were there. Genuinely useful, but shaped by memory and motive. Strongest when several accounts were collected separately and still match without anyone comparing notes.

A pointer only

Expert summaries and reporting

Analysis, journalism, and organisation reports. These point you toward the evidence - they are not the evidence themselves. Always ask what original proof they rest on.

Not proof

Opinion and assertion

A claim with nothing independent under it - a social post, an unsourced statement. Treat it as a lead to look into, never as something to repeat as fact.

One hard rule. Open or crowd-edited summary sites (like Wikipedia) and opinion blogs are never proof on their own. They can be a handy way to find the original source - but then you go and check that original source directly. If a claim can't be traced down to something real underneath, it isn't anchored to anything.

Putting it together

How sure should you be?

Once you've answered the five questions, you can place a claim into one of five honest levels of confidence. These are deliberately plain bands, not false precision - the point is to stop using vague words like "probably," which different people read completely differently.

Established

About as sure as we get. Several independent lines of strong proof all point the same way. Treat it as settled unless something extraordinary overturns it.

Probable

More likely true than not. Good evidence, with some gaps or a bit of disagreement. Reasonable to act on, but stay open to new information.

Contested

Genuinely unsettled. Real evidence on both sides. This does not mean both sides are equally strong - look at which one has the better original proof - but honestly, it isn't resolved.

Doubtful

Probably not true. The weight of evidence points against it. It leans on weak or interested sources, or doesn't trace back to anything solid.

Refuted

Shown to be false. Directly contradicted by strong evidence. Not merely unproven - actually disproven.

A note on honesty. Nothing is ever 100% certain or 0% certain - the scale never reaches the very ends. And the level tells you only how things stand right now. A claim can be weakly supported today and become solid tomorrow with one good piece of evidence, or the reverse. Always ask not just how confident to be, but how much that could change.

The one rule that holds it together

The mirror rule

Every test on this page only works if you apply it evenly. This is the rule that keeps you honest - including with yourself.

The mirror rule

Whatever standard you apply to a claim you dislike, apply exactly the same standard to a claim you want to be true.

If you would dismiss a source because it has an agenda, dismiss sources with the opposite agenda just as readily. If you would accept a report because it agrees with you, ask whether you would accept the same kind of report if it didn't. The test is simple: would I judge this the same way if it pointed to the opposite conclusion? If the answer is no, your standard is doing the work, not the evidence. This applies to this very website too - hold it to its own rules.

Watch it work

The five questions, on a real claim

Here is the whole method applied to something you have almost certainly seen a version of - a product claim backed by "independent research." Watch how fast it falls apart under the five questions.

The claim "Our software makes teams 40% more productive. This is backed by an independent study from Meridian Research Group."

Q1 What kind of proof is this?

It's a research report - which sits low on the ladder, a pointer toward evidence, not evidence itself. The real question is what the report is built on. Commissioned studies usually rest on surveys or on usage data supplied by the company. Neither is something an outsider can re-test.

Q2 Where does it come from?

Follow it back: the study was paid for by the software company. The company set the brief, picked who took part, and supplied the underlying data. The trail doesn't lead to an independent source - it loops straight back to the seller.

Q3 Do the sources really agree, or just repeat each other?

There look like three sources here - the company, the report, and "Meridian." But they share the same data, the same paymaster, and the same interest in a good result. That's not three independent confirmations. It's one. And what else could explain a 40% figure? Maybe only the best teams were measured. Maybe people worked harder because they knew they were being watched. The study would need to rule those out - it almost certainly doesn't.

Q4 Who gains, and what's left out?

The company gains directly from you believing the number - so it's an interested party making an assertion (Sign 03). And "40%" is suspiciously precise for this kind of research (Sign 07). We're shown the flattering figure; we're not shown the raw data, the teams that didn't improve, or how "productivity" was defined.

Q5 Has anyone serious tried to prove it wrong?

No independent team has tested this, because the only study was controlled by the company. Nothing here has survived hostile scrutiny - it has simply never faced any.

Doubtful

The claim looks like it has independent backing, but everything traces to a single interested source, and the precise figure isn't supported by anything an outsider can check. This isn't evidence that the software boosts productivity by 40%. It's evidence that the company paid for a study. Those are not the same thing. To believe the number, you'd want a genuinely independent test, with a comparison group, that someone else could repeat.
