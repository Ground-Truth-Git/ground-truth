An evidentiary framework

Know when you are being misled.

Propaganda does not announce itself. It works by manufacturing the appearance of evidence - repeating claims until they feel established, citing sources that trace back to one interested party, applying standards selectively depending on whose case they help. This framework names the techniques. Once you can name them, you can see them.

Section 01

Ten named failure modes

These are the specific techniques by which evidence is fabricated, inflated, or selectively applied. Each has a name so that when you see it, you can say exactly what is happening - and why it matters.

FM-01

Citation laundering

A claim passes through multiple outlets, each citing the previous one, with no source in the chain ever citing primary evidence. Repetition creates the appearance of broad support that does not exist.

How to spot it: trace every source to its origin. If the chain circles back or dead-ends at another secondary source, the claim has no anchor.

FM-02

Single-origin amplification

Multiple independent-seeming sources all trace back to the same single origin - one report, one dataset, one official statement. The sources are real; the independence is not.

How to spot it: ten sources citing the same underlying report are one piece of evidence, not ten.

FM-03

Interested-party assertion

A claim is sourced exclusively to a party with a direct stake in the finding being true - a government, a military, an advocacy organisation. The claim may be true, but it is not yet evidenced.

How to spot it: ask who benefits from this being believed. If the only sources are parties who benefit, the claim needs independent verification.

FM-04

Mandate creep

A body whose mandate covers X is cited as evidence for Y. A court issues provisional measures - this tells you a case was accepted for hearing, not that the claim is true. A committee passes a resolution - this tells you about voting composition, not about facts.

How to spot it: check what the cited body was actually asked to decide, and whether the claim matches that specific decision.

FM-05

Retracted or superseded source

A claim rests on a source that has since been corrected, retracted, or superseded by better evidence - but the original continues to circulate.

How to spot it: check whether the source is the most recent available, and whether subsequent research has revised the finding.

FM-06

Decontextualised statistic

A real number cited without the context that gives it meaning - without demographic breakdown, without comparison to comparable cases, without methodological notes. The number may be accurate; the claim built on it may not be.

How to spot it: ask what the full dataset shows, not just the headline figure.

FM-07

False precision

A claim stated with numerical specificity that implies rigorous measurement, when the underlying data is estimated, modelled, or given as a range. A single figure cited from a source that itself provides a wide uncertainty interval.

How to spot it: check whether the source gives a range or margin, and whether the claim has silently dropped it.

FM-08

Motive attribution without evidence

A claim about what a party intended, based only on the outcome rather than on documented orders, statements, or systematic patterns of behaviour. Intent is a separate factual question from outcome and requires its own direct evidence.

How to spot it: ask what evidence of intent exists - documents, orders, explicit statements - independent of the outcome itself.

FM-09

Isolated quote

A statement by one official, soldier, or commentator cited as representing the policy or intent of an entire government, organisation, or people.

How to spot it: ask whether the quote reflects documented official policy, or whether it is an individual statement by someone without authority to set policy.

FM-10

Asymmetric application

The evidentiary standard applied to one party in a dispute is not applied to the other. Sources accepted for one side are rejected for the other using different criteria.

How to spot it: ask whether you would accept this source type if it supported the opposite conclusion.

Section 02

Five-band evidence rating

Rather than reaching for words like "probably" or "seems to be" - words that different readers interpret at wildly different confidence levels - this framework maps claims to a numbered scale. The thresholds are borrowed from two mature traditions: United States intelligence doctrine on estimative probability, and the legal system's graded standards of proof.

Rating Confidence What it means
Established 95 - 99%+ Multiple independent lines of primary evidence converge. The current state of the field. Equivalent to "beyond reasonable doubt."
Probable 70 - 95% More likely true than not. Credible sourcing with some gaps or minor contrary evidence. Equivalent to "clear and convincing."
Contested 30 - 70% Credible evidence and argument exist on both sides. Genuinely uncertain. "Contested" does not mean both sides are equally supported - look at which side has the stronger primary sourcing.
Doubtful 5 - 30% The weight of evidence points against it. Primary sources do not support it. Relies on interested-secondary sources or outliers. Fails the civil standard.
Refuted <5% Directly contradicted by primary sources or forensic evidence. The claim is positively disproved, not merely unproven.
Evidential weight is separate from the band. Two claims can both sit at 50% confidence for opposite reasons - one finely balanced between two strong bodies of evidence, the other resting on almost nothing. The band tells you the current probability. The weight (heavy / moderate / light) tells you how far one new piece of evidence could move it. A light-weight claim at 50% could swing to 90% or 10% with a single document. A heavy-weight claim at 95% would need an extraordinary new finding to shift. Always ask both: what is the rating, and how much is it standing on?
Three permanent rules about confidence numbers. Never 0, never 100 - certainty is not on offer. Resolution is bounded - credences are stated in ranges, not precise points, because finer precision cannot be derived from most evidence and claiming it is itself a failure mode (FM-07, false precision). A confidence interval is a disciplined, auditable belief - not a measurement.

Section 03

Warrant, provenance, and independence

A claim is a chain from evidence to conclusion. Before asking whether a claim is true, ask what kind of evidence it rests on, whether that evidence survives being traced to its origin, and how many genuinely independent lines agree. Each question can collapse a claim that looked solid.

The warrant hierarchy - what kind of evidence is this?

The type of evidence sets the ceiling on how confident you can be. A claim can only be as strong as the evidence it stands on. Higher warrant types are harder to fabricate and harder for interested parties to revise.

1

Repeatable observation

Re-runnable experiments and measurements - radiocarbon dating, clinical trials with replication, established physics. The strongest because anyone can re-run the test and get the same result.

2

Physical and forensic evidence

Material records that exist independently of any account - forensic analysis, satellite imagery, archaeological findings, authenticated documents, DNA. Cannot be revised by a party with an interest in the outcome.

3

Contemporaneous record

Official documents, registers, and records created at the time of the events by parties present - contracts, censuses, court transcripts, contemporaneous news reports. Stronger than later reconstructions.

4

Reasoned inference from 1-3

Conclusions derived from hard evidence by stated logic. Valid when the logic is explicit and the underlying evidence is strong - but the inference itself adds uncertainty. The reasoning must be shown, not just the conclusion.

5

Testimony and confession

Direct accounts from witnesses or participants. Valuable but filtered by memory, motive, and sometimes coercion. Strongest when multiple accounts were collected separately and are mutually consistent without coordination.

6

Secondary synthesis

Expert analysis, investigative journalism, NGO reports. These are pointers toward primary evidence - not evidence themselves. Their only legitimate function is to lead you to a primary source. Always ask: what primary evidence does this rest on?

7

Assertion and opinion

Claims without independent evidential basis. Includes social media posts, unattributed statements, and claims by interested parties that cannot be traced to any primary source. Not evidence. Treat as a lead to investigate, not a fact to cite.

Hard rule on tertiary sources: Wikipedia, unsigned encyclopaedia-style overviews, aggregator sites, and advocacy blogs are never cited as authority. On contested topics they fail provenance, independence, and robustness at once - editable by interested parties, and typically one or two steps removed from whatever they summarise. Use them only as a finding aid to locate the primary source beneath, which is then verified and cited directly. A claim that cannot be re-grounded below the tertiary layer is unanchored.

Provenance - does it survive being traced to its origin?

Follow any source upstream. If it collapses onto a single, discredited, or fabricated origin, the credence collapses with it - regardless of how many outlets repeated it downstream. The pattern: thousands of citations, one retracted study underneath. Volume of repetition is not evidence of truth.

The provenance habit: before being impressed by how many sources say something, ask what they are all ultimately citing. If ten news outlets all reported the same claim on the same day, that is one piece of evidence - the original source - not ten. Trace it before trusting it.

Independence - how many genuinely separate lines agree?

Convergence of independent lines is what raises confidence. But independence must be demonstrated, not assumed. Two sources are only independent to the degree they share none of the following. If they share any of these, dependent repetitions add nothing to the evidential weight.

1

Origin

Same witness, document, or ultimate source

2

Method

Same technique, same blind spots

3

Data

Same underlying dataset

4

Institution

Same body, funder, or pipeline

5

Motive

Same incentive to reach the answer

Claim type caps the ceiling

Not all claims can be equally well established, regardless of how much evidence exists. The type of claim fixes the highest band it can reach - because no quantity of evidence turns an interpretation into an observation. Identify the claim type before assessing the evidence.

Claim type Example Ceiling
Existence / occurrence An event happened; a company made a product Established
Quantity / magnitude Roughly N cases occurred; market size is $X Established if independently measured
Attribution This data shows this pattern; this document says this Probable
Causal X caused Y; the policy produced this outcome Probable
Intent / mental state The decision was made in order to deceive Probable
Evaluative / definitional This constitutes fraud / negligence / misconduct Never scored whole - decompose first
On evaluative claims: a loaded label ("fraud", "negligence", "misconduct", "misleading") must be split into two questions - what factually happened (scored normally against evidence) and whether those facts meet the stated criteria of the term (argued openly, with the definition on the table). This stops a contested label from borrowing certainty from the facts beneath it.

The Mirror Rule

Every evidentiary standard applied to one party in a dispute must be applied identically to all other parties.

If you would dismiss a source as commercially interested, apply the same scrutiny to sources with the opposite commercial interest. If you would accept an NGO report on one side, apply the same acceptance threshold to NGO reports on the other. The test: would you apply this standard if it supported the opposite conclusion? If the answer is no, adjust until it is yes.

Section 04

The five-question test

A reader who never opens the full machinery can run the entire framework with five questions. If only one habit survives, make it question 2 - ask where a claim traces back to before being impressed by how many places you've seen it.

1

What kind of claim is this, and what is it ultimately based on?

Maps to: Claim type + Warrant hierarchy

2

Where does it trace back to?

Maps to: Provenance - follow upstream until you hit a primary source or a dead end

3

How many genuinely independent lines say it - and what else could explain the evidence?

Maps to: Independence (five dimensions) + rival hypotheses

4

Who benefits if I believe this, and what is being left out?

Maps to: Interest + assembly check (is the evidence representative, or a selected slice?)

5

Has anyone serious tried to disprove this and failed?

Maps to: Robustness / evidential weight - surviving hostile scrutiny is what locks confidence in
